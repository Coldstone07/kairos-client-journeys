Opening

Shoba is a Type 2 Helper who spent decades earning belonging through perfection and service. This conditioning came from South Indian cultural values, from being a mother to two children, from managing two full-time careers. For years, the entire structure depended on one thing: perfectionism. "Our family is one flat tire away from the entire thing imploding," she said in past seasons.

Then the architecture shifted. The children aged out of constant service. She left formal employment. Suddenly there was margin—space for things to go wrong without collapse. This margin revealed the truth her body had always known: the system was sturdier than she thought. Like Legos, not a house of cards.

In her March 4 session, Shoba discovered something radical: inefficiency is not failure. It is presence. It is spiritual practice. The daily cherry tomato run—driving to the store for one ingredient instead of optimizing weekly meal prep—became the symbol of her liberation. "That level of inefficiency is what makes us alive," she said, recognizing aliveness itself in what the perfectionist mind had called waste.

Margin is not laziness. It is the precondition for your true self to emerge.

Margin, Vocation & Inefficiency

Provocation → Dynamism → Liberation

The session opened with celebration. Shoba spoke of the dramatic shift in her life. When her children were small, she had structured every moment to prevent chaos. She worked two jobs, managed a household, maintained perfection across all domains. The entire system was predicated on one rule: never let anything slip.

"When I was when my kids were really small, I used to say our family is one flat tire away from the entire thing imploding...but now...there's margin. Like I didn't used to have margin and now I do."

What does margin mean? Shoba clarified: "The margin that I'm experiencing right now is that everything isn't predicated on everything being perfection or perfectionistic. Like there's room for mess in this life that didn't exist before." The entire architecture of safety had shifted. No longer dependent on control; now dependent on sturdiness. On trust. On the knowledge that the system can absorb disruption.

I love an inefficient life now. I'm loving that feeling. It's the privilege and the luxury of an imperfect life. — Shoba, Mar 4 Session
