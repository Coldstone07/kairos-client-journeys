Opening

A Question Waiting

In February 2026, Joanne Teh walked into a coaching container with a single, urgent question: "Where did all my anger go?"

She had spent her life as a peacemaker—the capable one, the calm one, the accommodator. In family systems terms, the identified peace-keeper. But beneath the surface, something was stirring. At a personal retreat in Peru, when someone attempted to kiss her without consent, she did what she always does: stayed quiet. Stayed peaceful. Stayed small.

But in the days after, the silence began to feel like a wound. Not to the circle—to herself. That contradiction is what brought her to coaching.

By not speaking up, I'm doing a dishonor to the circle. And by speaking up, I'm not short-changing myself. I'm allowing myself to feel whatever I need to feel.—Joanne, Session 1

Session 1: February 5, 2026

Where Did My Anger Go?

GK 55: Shadow → Gift | Enneagram 9w8 Recognition

The session began with framework introduction—not as fixed labels, but as mirrors. When Jatin explained the Enneagram Type 9, something landed for Joanne. "I've always been trying to be peaceful and chill and easygoing." The words felt true in her body.

The retreat incident surfaced as crucial context. In peacemaker mode, she had suppressed her anger to maintain harmony in the circle. But Kairos AI named something essential: "Anger usually is like a fire. It burns things. So when you're trying to build connection between people, any kind of extreme emotion other than positive emotions is generally problematic."

This is the Type 9 adaptation in its purest form. Keep peace. Suppress the fire. Become safe.

But Joanne's question was different: not "How do I get rid of my anger?" but "Where did it go? Why am I not feeling angry?" In that question lives the seed of Self—the knowing that anger has information, not a problem to solve.

Internal Family Systems

The System in Motion

Self ←→ Parts | Unblending the Protectors

Joanne's internal system has been organized around a single logic: peace through suppression.

At the retreat, when she was touched without consent, her system activated in sequence:

  1. The Unheard Child (Exile) felt the violation
  2. The Quiet Accommodator (Manager) immediately said: "Stay calm. Don't rock the boat."
  3. The Suppressor (Firefighter) followed: "Push the anger down before it disrupts the circle."
  4. Result: Silence. Safety. Self absent.

But something shifted in coaching. In the session container, Joanne accessed Self-energy. She spoke directly about the retreat. She named her pattern. She articulated the retreat boundary: "I could say I don't want to see this person in front of my face, and that's okay. I'm allowing myself to feel whatever I need to feel."

That is Self speaking—grounded, boundaried, truthful. Not yet consistent beyond the session, but beginning.

The IFS work ahead is not about eliminating the protective parts. They are doing their best with the only logic they learned. The work is inviting Self to become the leader—Self that can honor peace AND speak truth. Self that knows: boundaries don't break connections; they ground them.

Gene Keys Integration (Preliminary)*

From Victimization to Transcendence

GK 55 Vocation → GK 59 Radiance

*Note: These Gene Key assignments are thematic and emerged in session discussion but are not yet verified against birth data. They serve as exploratory maps for Joanne's journey.

GK 55: Vocation (The Gate of Abundance)

Shadow: Victimization — Joanne's life has been organized around the belief that her needs don't matter, that she must accommodate to have value. This is victimization in its deepest form: the surrender of her own power to keep others comfortable.

Gift: Freedom — The gift emerging is freedom to express, to assert, to claim her own knowing. At the retreat, when she spoke up despite the initial silence, she accessed this freedom. The gift says: "My voice matters. My boundaries are not violations; they are wisdom."

Siddhi: Transcendence — The ultimate realization: that claiming her power and speaking her truth is the highest service to the circle. Transcendence here means understanding that wholeness is what heals families, not fragmentation.

GK 59: Radiance (The Gate of Clarity)

Shadow: Dishonesty — Joanne named this herself: "By not speaking up, I'm doing a dishonor to the circle." Silence, when it serves suppression, is a form of dishonesty to self and system. The shadow is the belief that truth-telling will destroy what you're trying to protect.

Gift: Transparency — The gift is the capacity to be transparent about what is true for her—anger, boundary, need, desire. Transparency doesn't mean performing; it means letting others see the real you, the whole you.

Siddhi: Truth — The deepest realization is that truth-telling IS the highest service. When Joanne speaks her truth, she models for her family that voice matters, that wholeness is possible, that love doesn't require the erasure of self.

What Emerges

The Body's Knowing

Throat → Voice | Suppression → Expression

Joanne's pattern shows up first in the body. In session, coaches observed her smiling while discussing difficult topics—a somatic default that masks pain with pleasantness. Her speech patterns show word-searching, hesitation, the throat tight with practiced suppression. Even her voice, when she speaks of hard things, carries the weight of not-yet-landing anger.

The work ahead is fundamentally somatic:

  • Throat opening: Practicing speech in safe containers, feeling the difference between suppressed and expressed voice
  • Anger as sensation: Learning to locate anger in the body, not as problem but as signal. Where do you feel it? What is it protecting? What wisdom does it carry?
  • Ground and boundary: Standing with feet firmly rooted, practicing saying "No," practicing small boundary-settings daily
  • Sustained presence: Not accessing Self-energy just in the coaching session, but in daily life. In family. In the places where the peacekeeper habit was formed.

The body is not separate from the psyche. It is the psyche. Joanne's suppressed anger didn't disappear—it went into her throat, her shoulders, her smile. The path forward is bringing it home: feeling it, naming it, letting it teach her what matters.

What if the greatest gift Joanne can give her family is not her silence, but her wholeness? What if her anger, finally heard, becomes the permission others need to feel their own? The circle doesn't break when one voice speaks truth. It deepens.
