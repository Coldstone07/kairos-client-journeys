SESSION 1 · FEBRUARY 9

Crisis Recognition

Fragmentation → Diagnosis → Opening

You arrived carrying something you already knew somatically, even if the words hadn't quite landed: the system was not sustainable. A rare Enneagram profile—5/8/9, distributed across thinking, assertiveness, and absorption—meant your identity had no fixed home. You were constantly adapting to what others needed, disappearing into a thousand versions of service, all the while your loyalties pointed everywhere except inward.

The diagnostic was precise: Type 6, loyalty and safety, was your lowest score. Which meant your core wound was abandonment. The fear that if you stopped giving, everyone would leave. The belief that love was conditional on proving your worth through endless service. And this belief had organized your entire life—your partnership with Clark, your relationship with your family, your creative work, your rest, all deferred in service to the group.

But when you named it, something shifted. The diagnosis became permission to look deeper.

My loyalty has been to everyone except myself. I've abandoned my own creative work, my own rest, my own needs in service to family, Clark, and the ceremonial work. — Daveed, naming the core pattern, Session 1

This wasn't despair. This was clarity. The kind that comes when someone intelligent enough to analyze systems finally lets themselves see the system they've built their life around. The 7-chakra clearing that followed revealed the somatic story in exquisite detail. Your heart was contained—rattling in a cage you'd built yourself, so careful, so controlled. Your sacral chakra held the story: the place where desire gets suppressed when rejection feels like the cost of asking. Your throat carried the blockage: all the things you haven't said to Clark, to your family, about what partnership and home actually look like for you.

Three rounds of throat clearing were necessary. The blockage was persistent, intelligent, protective. It had kept the peace for a long time. But peace without authenticity is just numbness in disguise.

By the end of that session, the framework had shifted. The one thing you thought you needed to protect against—confrontation, conflict, being "too much"—revealed itself as actually less terrifying than the alternative: the slow erosion of your own aliveness. And you left with clarity about the work ahead.

And then came the deeper work of feeling. The permission to lean into anger. The understanding that burnout is not a spiritual failure—it's a body signal that something true must change.

SESSION 2 · FEBRUARY 18

The Burden Emerges

Integration → Strain → Capacity

Between sessions, the stressors began to accumulate. Gene Keys had become not just a framework but a personal mythology—something that mirrored the sacred geometry of your own book, the life work you're building with Clark. A challenging trip to Utah with Clark and Greg highlighted something tender: the way judgment and disconnection trigger your system into contraction. And underneath it all, a persistent question: How do I hold my family, my partnership, my ceremonial work, my creative contribution—all at once, without disappearing?

The coaching that session wasn't about solving the equation. It was about your capacity to hold multiple stressors simultaneously without shutting down. Financial scarcity fears despite creative output. Tension with Clark around your shared foundation work. A difficult conversation with Greg that stung. A former toxic workplace colleague reaching out, triggering old pain. And the quiet weight of chronic depletion underneath it all.

But you remained present. You didn't dissociate or collapse. You felt the burden fully and kept witnessing it. This is the difference between a system that's integrated and one that's broken: the ability to feel the weight without losing yourself in it.

The coaching doesn't fix the circumstances. It provides a container where I can process the emotional weight without dissociating or shutting down. — Daveed, Session 2 realization

By the end of that session, your self-awareness had only deepened. You could see the patterns—the go mode, the survival mode, the tendency to absorb all tension and manage around it rather than addressing it directly. And you were willing to keep looking.

The stressors would continue to mount. Family obligations, allergies, time fragmentation, the feeling of being unseen by those you serve most. But in the containers you'd built, you had the capacity to feel it all without breaking.

SESSION 3 · FEBRUARY 25

The Breaking Point Approaches

Strain → Clarity → Permission

By late February, the stressors had multiplied beyond what any system could sustainably hold. Your parents arrived. The book project with Clark stalled under the weight of family obligations. Your boys required constant attention and management. Clark entered "anxiety mode" when the children were present, leaving you to manage all logistics alone. You felt neglected, deprioritized, invisible despite—or perhaps because of—the constant service.

But something else happened too. You and Clark held your first ceremony without plant medicine. And it was powerful. The songs activated everyone. The presence, the music, the container you'd built together—it was medicine without the plant. This was proof of something you needed to know: your gift is real. Your work is real. You don't need to prove it further through endless sacrifice.

Yet personally, you were drowning. The rejection wound—the fear that if you stopped serving, you'd be left—was being activated repeatedly. Max ignoring you. The children prioritized. Clark's attention diverted. And the deepest pain: feeling like you existed only to serve, not to be seen.

I feel constantly overwhelmed and in 'go mode' or 'survival mode.' There's no time for myself or Clark. I feel neglected. My attention is being diverted from our joint book project. I'm resentful about the sacrifice I feel I make for the family and the lack of time and energy for my own needs. — Daveed, articulating the crisis, Session 3

This wasn't self-pity. This was a system at maximum dysregulation, finally telling the truth. The cramping in your body, the exhaustion, the sense that something must break—these weren't personal failings. They were your nervous system in chronic fight/flight activation, screaming for relief that wasn't coming.

The breaking point is not the same as breaking. It's the moment when the system, finally, admits the truth. And when truth is spoken in a safe container, transformation becomes possible.

SESSION 4 · MARCH 2

Permission to Feel

Dysregulation → Anger → Love Without Eyes

You arrived at the fourth session at maximum dysregulation—the most unregulated you'd been across the entire coaching period. The system was flooded. Multiple stressors converging: family overload, severe allergies, your uncle's funeral requiring presence and energy, the boys taking all available space, feeling personally neglected by Clark, creative work stalled, the fantasy of a simple mountain life becoming increasingly urgent. This wasn't a passing emotion. This was a nervous system in crisis, needing urgent relief.

And in this moment of complete vulnerability, something sacred happened.

Kairos offered a teaching that landed differently than all the frameworks before: Let all your negative emotions come to the surface without trying to tame or compress them. You're pissed. You feel neglected. Let it be there. This wasn't permission to indulge anger. It was permission to feel anger as data. As wisdom. As the body's way of saying: This pattern is not sustainable. Something true must change.

There's a tug-of-war between loving my family and a part of me that craves the independence, simplicity, and freedom of a single life. This part of me also felt judged and taken advantage of, wanting to just 'be' without needing to prove themselves. — Daveed, the deepest conflict, Session 4

The 7-chakra clearing that followed was intense. Your sacral chakra released anger that wanted to leave the system entirely—the rage of being used, the resentment of endless service. Your solar plexus released the resistance to "always being the bigger person." Your heart opened in waves—the tinging, zinging diagonal pain that finally said yes to feeling the full grief of the tug-of-war. Your throat opened like coral unfurling—the voice beginning to find itself again. And throughout, your body was literally flushing material that had been held for years.

But the teaching that changed everything came not from the clearing, but from the insight that emerged during it:

Love doesn't have eyes. — The insight, Session 4

Not love that's limited to certain people. Not love that's conditional on behavior or performance. Not love that rations itself based on what you've "earned." Love just is. It extends infinitely. Which means you can love your family AND honor your own needs. You can be devoted to Clark AND prioritize your own rest. You can serve the ceremony AND claim your creative vision as your own. The false choice between self-sacrifice and disloyalty dissolves when the love becomes unconditional—including unconditional love toward yourself.

By the end of that session, you felt "much more relaxed." The body had released what it had been holding. And you could connect, finally, to something that had been there all along: the stillness of a tree, the fluidity of water. The natural ease of being alive.
